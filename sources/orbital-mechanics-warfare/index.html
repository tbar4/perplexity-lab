Why physics breaks every fighter pilot reflex in space

Module 01 · Clohessy–Wiltshire Equations

Thrust forward. Fall behind.

The chaser (cyan) sits five kilometers behind the target (amber). Both orbit at 400 km altitude. Instinct says: throttle forward to close the gap. Click Thrust Prograde and watch.

Adding velocity raises the chaser into a higher, slower orbit. Period lengthens. The chaser drifts backward in the local frame. To actually catch the target, you must thrust retrograde, drop into a faster lower orbit, then rise to meet it.

Equations: Clohessy & Wiltshire (1960). Target-centered LVLH frame, linearized for small separations.

Module 02 · Lagrange Points

The high ground that slides away.

Five points balance Earth–Moon gravity. Strategists call them cislunar high ground. But not all balance is equal. L1, L2, L3 are saddle points: stable along one axis, unstable along another. L4 and L5 sit at the bottom of effective potential wells, genuinely stable.

Click a point, then Perturb to nudge the spacecraft. Watch which ones come home and which ones drift forever. A weapons platform parked at L1 burns fuel just to exist.

Simplified circular restricted three-body problem. JWST orbits L2 with continuous station-keeping burns.

Module 03 · Line-of-Sight Windows

Ninety minutes. Ten of them useful.

A satellite in LEO completes one orbit every ~90 minutes. From any fixed ground station, the satellite is only above the horizon for roughly 10 minutes per pass. The rest of the time, Earth occults the link.

This simulation runs a compressed 90-minute orbit. The green sector marks line-of-sight to the ground station. Red marks occlusion. A defender has perhaps six brief engagement windows per day against any single LEO target.

Single ground station, 400 km circular LEO, 5° minimum elevation mask. Real constellations relay around the gaps.

Module 04 · Hohmann vs. Direct Pursuit

Patience beats brute force.

Target orbits 1000 km above the interceptor. The Hohmann transfer uses two small burns, half an orbit apart, raising apogee then circularizing. Cheapest possible two-impulse maneuver.

The direct intercept burns radially toward the target. Fast, but bleeds enormous Δv and arrives with high closing velocity. Press Launch and watch both run simultaneously.

Hohmann ≈ 60% less Δv than a direct radial burn for this scenario, at the cost of ~50 minutes vs. ~10.
